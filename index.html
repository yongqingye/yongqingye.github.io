My name is Yongqing Ye [joŋtɕʰi≈ã je]. I am a computational linguist and speech scientist, employing both behavioral and computational methods to explore human phonological knowledge and its intersections with phonetics and performance systems.
My research is driven by two mutually reinforcing goals: (1) to examine how humans make use of phonological knowledge in speech perception and production and (2) to use the observations and insights drawn from the performance system to inform our theories on phonological representations and sound change.
I work with both experimental and corpus data. My current research project centered on developing Bayesian models to investigate the temporal dynamics of vowel nasalization perception. Specifically, I have focused on training acoustic models with data from a range of languages with varying nasalization patterns, such as Hindi, Contemporary American English, and Peninsular Spanish and building perceptual models to delve into how how different sources of knowledge, such as acoustic cues, phoneme likelihood, and underspecification, affect listeners' decision-making during vowel nasalization perception. The goal is to create formal and explicit models of both the acoustic/auditory processes involved in perception and the perceptual computations applied to these acoustic models, allowing us to precisely define, articulate, and evaluate the underlying assumptions, decisions, and predictions that shape our phonological and perceptual theories.
